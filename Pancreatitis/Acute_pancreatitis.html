ESPEN practical guideline on clinical nutrition in ACUTE pancreatitis

Arvanitakis. M, et al. Clinical Nutrition, February 2024

Gustavo Santos Paiva Laender Moura

2025-05-27

1. Introduction

Acute pancreatitis (AP)

  • Prevalence: Most common acute gastrointestinal disease requiring hospital admission.
  • Prognosis: Favorable in ~80% of cases.
  • Severe Form: Acute Necrotizing Pancreatitis (ANP) affects ~20%, with:
    • 38% early organ failure
    • 38% requiring intervention
    • 15% mortality rate
  • Metabolic Impact: High catabolism—nutritional support is crucial.
  • Nutritional Strategy:
    • Enteral Nutrition (EN) proven superior to Parenteral Nutrition (PN).
    • This led to a paradigm shift in ANP management over the past decade.
  • Current Research Gaps:
    • Optimal timing, route, and type of EN.
    • Role of oral refeeding still under investigation.

2. Methodology Overview

  • Content:
    • 42 recommendations (19 for acute and 23 for chronic pancreatitis)
    • 6 statements
  • Development Process:
    • Systematic literature search (Dec 1, 2018) based on 31 PICO questions.
    • Evidence graded using SIGN system.
    • Recommendations classified into four grades: A, B, 0, GPP.
  • Consensus:
    • Achieved through a multistage process with % agreement recorded.

Recomendation 1 (Nutritional risk)

Patients with acute pancreatitis should be considered at moderate to high nutritional risk, because of the catabolic nature of the disease and because of the impact of the nutritional status for disease development.

(S1, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Mild to Moderate AP:
    • Most patients recover fully within a week.
    • Oral feeding is usually feasible within a few days.
  • Severe AP:
    • Up to 60% develop gut-barrier dysfunction, increasing risk of:
      • Bacterial translocation
      • Infected necrosis
    • Severe catabolism places these patients at high nutritional risk.
  • Other At-Risk Groups:
    • Malnourished patients, even with mild AP, due to pre-existing deficits.
    • Patients with high alcohol consumption, often chronically malnourished.

Recomendation 2 (Nutritional Risk)

All patients with predicted mild to moderate acute pancreatitis should be screened using validated screening methods such as the Nutritional Risk Screening – 2002 (NRS-2002); however, the patients with predicted severe acute pancreatitis should always be considered at nutritional risk.

  • Screening Tools:
    • NRS 2002 helps identify patients at nutritional risk.
    • Validated in hospitalized and critically ill patients [13, 14, 15, 16].
    • Not specifically validated for AP patients [17].
  • BMI Considerations:
    • Body mass index (BMI) is useful for risk assessment:
      • Low BMI: Indicates pre-existing malnutrition.
      • Obesity: Increases risk for severe AP, acting as a disease severity-related nutritional risk [18].

Recomendation 3: Oral feeding with low fat soft diet

Oral feeding shall be offered as soon as clinically tolerated and independent of serum lipase concentrations in patients with predicted mild AP.

(R2, grade A, strong consensus, 100 %)

Early Oral Feeding in Mild to Moderate AP

  • Evidence from 4 RCTs:
    • Early oral feeding is safe and well-tolerated.
    • Leads to shorter hospital stay compared to conventional strategies.
    • Conventional feeding (not recommended) waits for:
      • Enzyme reduction
      • Pain resolution
      • Return of bowel function
  • Key Finding:
    • One RCT showed that feeding is safe regardless of serum lipase levels [8, 19, 20, 21, 22].

Recomendation 4

Low-fat, soft oral diet shall be used when reinitiating oral feeding in patients with mild acute pancreatitis.

(R3, grade A, strong consensus, 100 %)

  • Soft diets improve caloric intake and are as well tolerated as clear liquid diets [22, 23, 24].
  • A meta-analysis confirmed early oral feeding is feasible and reduces length of stay.
  • 16.3% of patients may experience feeding intolerance [26].
  • Special Consideration – Hyperlipidemia Induced AP:
    • Accounts for 4–10% of AP cases.
    • Associated with worse prognosis [27, 28, 29].
    • Management includes:
      • Nil by mouth for 24–48h initially.
      • Dietary modifications and lipid-lowering medications.
      • In-hospital treatment with insulin, heparin, and possibly plasmapheresis.

Recomendation 5: Enteral nutrition in case of intolerance to oral feeding

In patients with AP and inability to feed orally, enteral nutrition shall be preferred to parenteral nutrition.

(R4, grade A, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Physiological Benefits of EN:
    • Preserves gut mucosa integrity.
    • Stimulates intestinal motility.
    • Prevents bacterial overgrowth.
    • Increases splanchnic blood flow [9].
  • Evidence Base:
    • 12 RCTs and 11 systematic reviews/meta-analyses, including a Cochrane-standard meta-analysis, confirm EN is:
      • Safe and well-tolerated in severe AP.
      • Reduces:
        • Complications
        • Multi-organ failure
        • Infections
        • Mortality compared to Parenteral Nutrition [30-40].
  • Key Findings – Al-Omran et al. Meta-analysis:
    • Compared to PN, EN reduces:
      • Mortality by 50% (OR 0.50)
      • Infection rate (OR 0.39)
      • Multi-organ failure (OR 0.55)
      • Need for surgery (OR 0.44)
    • In severe AP only, mortality reduced by >80% (OR 0.18).
  • Recent Confirmations:
    • Latest meta-analyses in critically ill AP patients show EN significantly reduces:
      • Mortality (RR 0.36)
      • Multiple organ failure (RR 0.39) [38].

Recomendation 6: EN Timing

Enteral nutrition should be started early, within 24–72 h of admission, in case of intolerance to oral feeding

(R5, grade B, strong consensus, 92 %)

  • Meta-Analyses Findings:
    • Early EN within 24h [41, 42, 43] or 48h [44, 45, 46] is:
      • Safe, well-tolerated, and beneficial.
      • Reduces mortality, organ failure, and infections.
    • Limitation: Most included studies had PN as the control group, not delayed EN.
  • RCTs with Different Findings:
    • Two RCTs [43, 47] showed:
      • No difference in major infection or mortality between:
        • Early EN (within 24h) vs.
        • Oral diet after 72h.
  • Optimal Timing Insight:
    • A prospective cohort study [48] suggests:
      • Day 3 after admission is the optimal cut-off for EN.
      • Associated with:
        • Reduced secondary infections.
        • Better nutritional status.
        • Improved tolerance.

Recomendation 7: Formula selection

In patients with acute pancreatitis a standard polymeric diet shall be used.

(R6, grade A, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Formula Types:
    • Semi-elemental and polymeric formulas are:
      • Safe, feasible, and well-tolerated in AP patients.
  • Key Findings:
    • Small RCT (30 patients) found both formulas effective, but semi-elemental diets showed:
      • Shorter length of stay (23 ± 2 vs. 27 ± 1 days, p = 0.006).
      • Better weight maintenance [49].
    • Meta-analysis by Petrov et al. (428 patients) showed:
      • No difference in tolerance, infection, or mortality between formulas [50].
    • Larger meta-analysis (15 trials, 1376 participants) also found:
      • No evidence favoring any specific formula [51].
  • Clinical Consideration:
    • In patients with malabsorption (often in severe AP), semi-elemental diets may be preferable.

Recomendation 8: EN Route

If enteral nutrition is required in patients with acute pancreatitis, it should be administered via a nasogastric tube. Administration via a nasojejunal tube should be preferred in case of digestive intolerance, such as pain and vomiting.

(R7, grade B, strong consensus, 95 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • Use a nasogastric tube for enteral nutrition in acute pancreatitis.
    • Switch to nasojejunal tube if there is digestive intolerance (e.g., pain, vomiting).
  • Evidence Summary:
    • 3 RCTs [52, 53, 54] found no differences in:
      • Tolerance
      • Complications
      • Mortality between nasogastric and nasojejunal routes.
    • 4 meta-analyses [55, 56, 57, 58] confirm:
      • Nasogastric feeding is safe, feasible, and effective.
      • No increase in mortality, complications, or hospital stay compared to nasojejunal feeding.
  • Practical Considerations:
    • Nasogastric tubes:
      • Easier, cheaper, and more convenient.
    • Digestive intolerance (~15%) may occur due to:
      • Delayed gastric emptying.
      • Gastric outlet syndrome.
    • In such cases, nasojejunal feeding is preferred.
  • Limitations:
    • Trials included small sample sizes and varied definitions of severe AP.

Recomendation 9: PN in case of intolerance of EN

PN should be administered in patients with acute pancreatitis who do not tolerate enteral nutrition or who are unable to tolerate targeted nutritional requirements, or if contraindications for EN exist.

(R8, grade GPP, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • PN should be used in patients with acute pancreatitis who:
      • Cannot tolerate enteral nutrition (EN).
      • Cannot meet nutritional targets via EN.
      • Have contraindications for EN.
  • Rationale:
    • EN is the preferred route due to superior outcomes.
    • PN is reserved for cases of EN failure or contraindications.
  • Contraindications for EN include:
    • Bowel obstruction.
    • Abdominal compartment syndrome.
    • Paralytic ileus.
    • Mesenteric ischemia [59].
  • Clinical Insight:
    • About 20% of severe AP patients present with complications that are absolute or relative contraindications for EN, similar to other critically ill populations [16].

Recomendation 10: Glutamine in PN

When enteral nutrition is not feasible or contraindicated and parenteral nutrition is indicated, parenteral glutamine should be supplemented at 0.20 g/kg per day of l-glutamine. Otherwise, there is no role for immunonutrition in severe acute pancreatitis.

(R16, grade B, strong consensus, 94 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • When EN is not feasible and PN is indicated, add parenteral glutamine at 0.20 g/kg/day of L-glutamine.
    • No role for other forms of immunonutrition in severe AP.
    • (Grade B, strong consensus, 94%)
  • Evidence Summary:
    • Initial meta-analysis (11 RCTs):
      • Mixed results for antioxidants; benefits mainly from glutamine [60].
    • Cochrane Review:
      • Very low-quality evidence — no effect on mortality [61].
    • Glutamine-specific meta-analyses:
      • 4 meta-analyses consistently showed:
        • Reduced infection rates and mortality.
        • Benefits observed only with PN, not EN [62, 63, 64].
      • Improved serum albumin and reduced C-reactive protein.
  • Limitations:
    • High risk of bias due to:
      • Small sample sizes.
      • Study heterogeneity.
      • Confounding factors.

Recomendation 11: Probiotics NOT recommended

Probiotics cannot be recommended in patients with severe acute pancreatitis.

(R17, grade 0, consensus, 89 %)

  • Recommendation: Probiotics are contraindicated in severe AP due to lack of benefit and potential harm.
  • Evidence Summary:
    • Meta-analysis of 6 RCTs (536 patients) showed no benefit on:
      • Pancreatic infection rates
      • Overall infections
      • Operation rates
      • Hospital stay
      • Mortality [65].
    • Significant heterogeneity in:
      • Strain types
      • Dosages
      • Treatment durations
    • One RCT reported increased mortality in the probiotic group despite similar infection rates [66].

Recomendation 12: Pancreatic enzymes NOT recommended

Pancreatic enzymes should not be supplemented generally except in patients with obvious pancreatic exocrine insufficiency.

(R18, grade B, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Evidence Summary:
    • Only 2 small RCTs (78 patients total):
      • Kahl et al.:
        • 20/56 had low fecal elastase (indicating PEI).
        • Slight improvement with enzymes, not statistically significant [67].
      • Patankar et al.:
        • No significant difference in lab or clinical outcomes [68].
  • Clinical Guidance:
    • Do not prescribe enzymes routinely.
    • Indicated only if:
      • Documented PEI.
      • Malabsorption with steatorrhea.

Recomendation 13: Oral feeding with low fat soft diet after minimally invasive necrosectomy

Oral food intake in patients undergoing minimally invasive necrosectomy is safe and feasible and should be initiated in the first 24 h after the procedure, if the clinical state (hemodynamic stability, septic parameters, gastric emptying) of the patient allows it.

(R9, grade GPP, strong consensus, 95 %)

  • Clinical Context:
    • Patients with acute necrotizing pancreatitis (ANP) have higher risks of:
      • Multiple organ failure
      • Infected necrosis
      • Mortality [69].
  • Surgical Advances:
    • Minimally invasive techniques (step-up approach) are now standard, replacing open surgery due to better outcomes [70, 71].
  • Nutritional Approach:
    • In a comparative trial [72]:
      • Oral nutrition was attempted first.
      • If not tolerated → nasojejunal EN.
      • If EN contraindicated → parenteral nutrition (PN).
  • Conclusion:
    • Early oral feeding is safe and feasible following minimally invasive necrosectomy, when clinically appropriate.

Recomendation 14: EN in case of intolerance to oral feeding or insufficiency of oral feeding

In patients undergoing minimally invasive necrosectomy who are unable to be fed orally, EN is indicated via nasojejunal as preferred route.

(R10, grade B, strong consensus, 91 %)

  • In patients unable to tolerate oral feeding after minimally invasive necrosectomy, use enteral nutrition (EN) via nasojejunal tube as the preferred route.
  • Evidence Summary:
    • Supported by small series and trials showing that nasojejunal feeding is safe and feasible.
    • RCT by Bakker et al. [43]:
      • No difference in outcomes between early EN (nasojejunal within 24h) and oral feeding after 72h.
      • Included various procedures: percutaneous drainage, endoscopic/surgical necrosectomy.
    • Retrospective study (37 patients):
      • Oral feeding within 24–48h after laparoscopic transgastric necrosectomy was safe and feasible [64].
    • Prospective study (40 patients):
      • Nasojejunal EN preferred after video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement.
      • PN used only if EN was not tolerated [65].
  • Conclusion:
    • Nasojejunal feeding is the preferred option when oral intake is not possible following minimally invasive necrosectomy.

Recomendation 15: Complementary or exclusive PN in case of intolerance to EN

Parenteral nutrition is indicated in patients undergoing minimally invasive necrosectomy who do not tolerate EN or who are unable to tolerate targeted nutritional requirements, or if there exist contraindications for enteral nutrition.

(R11, grade GPP, strong consensus, 94 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • Parenteral nutrition (PN) is indicated for patients after minimally invasive necrosectomy when:
      • Enteral nutrition (EN) is not tolerated.
      • EN does not meet nutritional targets.
      • Contraindications to EN are present.
    • (Grade GPP, strong consensus, 94%)
  • Clinical Insight:
    • Consistent with previous recommendations (10 and 11).
    • PN is a backup strategy, not the first choice.
    • Use PN to ensure adequate nutritional support when EN is not feasible or fails.
  • Conclusion:
    • In post-necrosectomy patients, PN is essential if EN is not possible to meet the patient’s metabolic and recovery needs.

Recomendation 16: Intra-abdominal pressure monitoring

In patients with severe acute pancreatitis and intra-abdominal pressure < 15 mmHg, early enteral nutrition shall be initiated via nasojejunal, as the preferred route, or nasogastric tube. Intra-abdominal pressure and the clinical condition of patients during enteral nutrition shall be monitored continuously.

(R12, grade A, strong consensus, 91 %)

  • For patients with severe acute pancreatitis (AP) and intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) <15 mmHg:
    • Early enteral nutrition (EN) should be initiated.
    • Nasojejunal tube is preferred, but nasogastric is acceptable.
    • Monitor IAP and clinical status continuously during EN.
  • Rationale and Evidence:
    • While EN reduces mortality and infections, it may elevate IAP, risking abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS) [73, 74].
    • Observational study (274 patients):
      • 103 developed ACS.
      • EN intolerance was higher in patients with grade III–IV intra-abdominal hypertension (IAH); 59% required PN [75].
    • RCT (60 patients) found:
      • Early EN when IAP <15 mmHg prevents progression to IAH [76].
  • Conclusion:
    • Start EN early if IAP <15 mmHg.
    • Monitor IAP closely to avoid complications related to rising intra-abdominal pressure.

Recomendation 17: EN in Severe AP with IAP >15 mmHg

In patients with severe acute pancreatitis and intra-abdominal pressure > 15 mmHg, enteral nutrition should be initiated via nasojejunal route starting at 20 mL/h, increasing the rate according to the tolerance. Temporary reduction or discontinuation of enteral nutrition should be considered when intra-abdominal pressure values further increase under enteral nutrition.

(R13, grade B, strong consensus, 94 %)

  • In patients with severe acute pancreatitis (AP) and intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) >15 mmHg:
    • Start enteral nutrition (EN) via nasojejunal tube at 20 mL/h.
    • Gradually increase the rate based on tolerance and IAP measurements.
    • Consider temporary reduction or discontinuation if IAP increases during EN.
  • Clinical Rationale:
    • Patients with intra-abdominal hypertension (IAH) frequently exhibit digestive intolerance.
    • Cautious initiation of EN is critical:
      • Begin at 20 mL/h
      • Adjust based on IAP trends and patient tolerance [77].
  • Conclusion:
    • Careful, gradual EN delivery via nasojejunal route is recommended in severe AP with elevated IAP, with ongoing IAP monitoring to guide adjustments.

Recomendation 18: PN in Severe AP with IAP >20 mmHg or ACS

In patients with severe acute pancreatitis and intra-abdominal pressure > 20 mmHg or in the presence of acute compartment syndrome, enteral nutrition should be (temporarily) stopped and parenteral nutrition should be initiated.

(R14, grade GPP, strong consensus, 94 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • In patients with severe acute pancreatitis (AP) and:
      • Intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) >20 mmHg, or
      • Abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS) →
    • Stop enteral nutrition (EN) temporarily.
    • Initiate total parenteral nutrition (PN).
    • (Grade GPP, strong consensus, 94%)
  • Clinical Rationale:
    • IAP >20 mmHg or ACS presents critical risk where EN may worsen the condition.
    • Switching to PN prevents further pressure elevation and complications [74].
  • Conclusion:
    • In severe AP with critically high IAP or ACS, EN must be interrupted, and PN is mandatory until IAP improves.

Recomendation 19: Severe AP with Open abdomen

In patients with severe acute pancreatitis and open abdomen, enteral nutrition should be administered, at least in a small amount. If required for achievement of nutritional requirements, supplementary or total parenteral nutrition should be added.

(R15, grade B, strong consensus, 97 %)

  • Recommendation:
    • In patients with severe acute pancreatitis (AP) and an open abdomen:
      • Enteral nutrition (EN) should be administered, at least in small amounts.
      • Use supplementary or total parenteral nutrition (PN) if EN alone does not meet nutritional needs.
  • Clinical Rationale:
    • Decompressive laparotomy (laparostomy) is required in up to 74% of patients with abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS) during AP [78].
    • Cohort studies show EN is generally feasible and safe, though digestive intolerance rates range from 48% to 67% [79–84].
    • Two studies reported that early EN in open abdomen patients leads to:
      • Higher fascial closure rates.
      • Lower fistula rates.
      • Reduced nosocomial infections.
      • Lower hospital costs [83, 84].
  • Conclusion:
    • Even in the presence of an open abdomen, EN should be maintained whenever possible, supported by PN if needed, due to significant clinical benefits.
